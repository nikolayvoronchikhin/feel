feel

feel is a simple weather-status watchface. Useful for active atheletes, meteorologists, weather nerds, and anyone who would like to know the relationship between temperature and dew point.

We perceive the weather based on how hot or how cold the temperature is. Yet certain environmental conditions make the temperature feel different. In particular, the dew point often affects to what extent the temperature is perceived or felt.

Air is composed of water vapour. The total amount of energy is constant at any given time; hence, the rate of energy that is produced and released must be at equilibrium as well. Humans sweat as a result of having used energy, and how much water vapour is in the air will have affected how much sweat can evaporate — and how comfortable we may be given the weather conditions.

On a typical summer's day in San Francisco, for instance, the temperature is 21C (70F) with a dew point of 14C (57F). Quite comfortable weather, with the sea-breeze especially. Compare this to Hong Kong in summer, with a temperature of 32C (90F) and dew point of 26C (79F). Very much like walking in soup, and the winds are calm, usually, adding to the effect.

In general,

  • If the air is saturated, there is less evaporation from the skin, and more vapour pressure is exerted on the human body. Hence the air will feel 'heavy', 'muggy', 'moist', 'sultry', or any related terms.
  • If the air isn't saturated, there is more evaporation from the skin, and less vapour pressure is exerted on the human body. Hence the air will feel 'light', 'dry', 'comfortable', or any related terms.

feel will take the weather data, courtesy of OpenWeatherMap and extract the temperature and relative humidity. Through a rather complicated formula, the dew point is computed using the temperature and relative humidity. Use feel for a week and let me know how it is.
